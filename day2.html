DAY 2

How Startups Make Money

Business Models & Revenue Streams

💡
Tip for Beginners
Don't build first, validate first. Talk to 20 potential users before writing a single line of code. Most failures happen because people build what nobody wants.

🎯 Business Model vs Revenue Model

Business Model
The overall strategy of how a company creates, delivers, and captures value.
Revenue Model
The specific way a company makes money from its business model.
Example: Zomato
Business Model: Connect restaurants with customers through digital platform

Revenue Models:
• Commission from restaurants
• Delivery fees from customers
• Zomato Gold subscriptions
• Restaurant advertising

💰 The 8 Major Revenue Models

1. Commission-Based

How it works: Take percentage of every transaction

Examples
• Flipkart: 10-20% from sellers
• Ola/Uber: 20-25% per ride
• Airbnb: 3% from hosts, 14% from guests

Pros: Scales with transaction volume
Cons: Need high volume to be profitable

2. Subscription Model

How it works: Users pay recurring fee (monthly/yearly)

Examples
• Netflix: ₹199-649/month
• Spotify: ₹119/month
• Amazon Prime: ₹1,499/year

Variants: Freemium, Tiered pricing, Usage-based
Pros: Predictable revenue
Cons: Must deliver constant value

3. Marketplace Model

How it works: Connect buyers and sellers, earn from both

Examples
• OLX: Free listings, paid promotions
• Meesho: Commission from suppliers
• UrbanClap: Service fee from professionals

Revenue Sources: Listing fees, Featured placements, Transaction commissions, Advertising

4. Advertising Model

How it works: Offer free service, make money from ads

Examples
• Google: Search free, ads generate $200B+
• Facebook/Instagram: Free platform, ad revenue
• YouTube: Free videos, ads + premium

Reality: Only works at massive scale (millions of users)

5. SaaS (Software as a Service)

How it works: Sell software on subscription to businesses

Examples
• Zoho: ₹125-1,250/user/month
• Freshworks: Customer support software
• Razorpay: Payment processing + fees

Advantage: B2B customers pay more than consumers

6. Freemium Model

How it works: Basic version free, advanced features paid

Examples
• LinkedIn: Free profile, Premium ₹1,600/month
• Canva: Free templates, Pro ₹500/month
• Grammarly: Free checks, Premium for advanced

Reality: 2-5% free users convert to paid. Need millions of users.

7. Transaction Fee Model

How it works: Charge per transaction processed

Examples
• Razorpay: 2% per transaction
• PayTM: Merchant fees
• PhonePe: UPI free (monetize other services)

Math: Process ₹100Cr/month × 2% fee = ₹2Cr revenue

8. Direct Sales (D2C)

How it works: Manufacture and sell directly, no middlemen

Examples
• Boat: Earphones, speakers (₹3,000Cr+ revenue)
• Mamaearth: Beauty products
• Lenskart: Eyewear

Advantage: Higher margins (40-60% vs 10-20% in retail)

🧮 Unit Economics Explained

Definition: Revenue and costs associated with a single unit of business

Food Delivery Example
Revenue per order:
• Food cost: ₹300
• Delivery fee: ₹40
• Platform commission (30%): ₹90
• Total revenue: ₹130

Costs per order:
• Delivery partner: ₹60
• Tech infrastructure: ₹10
• Customer support: ₹5
• Marketing: ₹80
• Total cost: ₹155

Unit Economics: -₹25 (LOSS)

Many startups lose money per transaction initially, betting on future profitability at scale.

📊 CAC vs LTV - The Critical Ratio

CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)
How much it costs to get one customer

Example: ₹50,000 spent on ads → 500 signups = ₹100 CAC
LTV (Lifetime Value)
Total revenue from one customer over their lifetime

Example: ₹500/month × 8 months = ₹4,000 LTV
The Golden Rule

LTV should be 3x or more than CAC

  • LTV ₹4,000 vs CAC ₹100 = Excellent (40x)
  • LTV ₹4,000 vs CAC ₹3,000 = Risky (1.3x)
  • LTV ₹4,000 vs CAC ₹5,000 = Unsustainable

🔥 The Burn Rate Concept

Definition: How fast a startup spends money (monthly)

Monthly Expenses Breakdown
• Salaries (5 people): ₹5,00,000
• Office rent: ₹50,000
• Cloud services: ₹30,000
• Marketing: ₹2,00,000
• Miscellaneous: ₹20,000

Total Burn Rate: ₹8,00,000/month

If funding raised: ₹2 Crore
Runway: 25 months

Must achieve profitability or raise more funding before runway ends.

📈 Real Company Revenue Models

Zepto (Quick Commerce)

Model: Commission + delivery fees
Revenue per order: ₹50-80
Orders: 5L+ daily
Monthly revenue: ~₹100Cr+
Status: Loss-making (high operational costs)

Razorpay (Fintech)

Model: Transaction fees (2%)
Processes: ₹10L Cr+ annually
Revenue: ~₹2,000Cr/year
Status: Profitable in some quarters

Boat (D2C)

Model: Direct sales
Average price: ₹2,000
Units sold: 50L+/year
Revenue: ₹3,000Cr+
Profit margin: 8-10%

Cred (Fintech)

Model: Evolving (lending partnerships)
Revenue: ₹2,000Cr+
Burns: ₹1,500Cr/year
Status: Not profitable (focus on user growth)

📉 Why Many Startups Don't Make Money

  • Market Capture Strategy: Intentionally lose money to dominate market (Example: Jio lost ₹50,000Cr initially)
  • Winner-Takes-All Markets: Race to grow fast, only top 2-3 survive (Ola vs Uber)
  • High CAC: Spending more to acquire customers than they're worth
  • Poor Unit Economics: Costs don't improve with scale
  • Wrong Revenue Model: Monetizing wrong aspect of business

📌 Key Takeaways
  • Revenue model determines how startup makes money
  • Unit economics must work (revenue > cost per transaction)
  • CAC must be much lower than LTV (ideally 1:3 ratio)
  • Burn rate determines survival runway
  • Not all startups need immediate profitability
  • Indian startups typically take 5-7 years to profit
  • SaaS and D2C are naturally more profitable
  • Marketplaces and consumer apps take longer
